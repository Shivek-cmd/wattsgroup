Building a Multi-Vertical Conglomerate: Lessons from Canada

By Ritesh Watts Oct 18, 2024 9 min read

Building a multi-vertical conglomerate is one of the hardest things you can do in business. Every investor, advisor, and MBA textbook will tell you to focus. Pick one thing. Get great at it. Then scale. That advice is correct for most founders. But for a specific type of builder — one who sees interconnections between industries that others miss — the conglomerate model can create something far more powerful than any single venture could achieve alone.

At Watts Group, we've built eight companies across six verticals in five years. AI, real estate, immigration, education, entertainment, and community development. Each venture reinforces the others. Our immigration clients need real estate. Our education students need immigration services. Our AI tools power every company in the portfolio. This isn't diversification for its own sake. It's a deliberate ecosystem.

Here are the lessons we've learned building it.

Why the Conglomerate Model Still Works

The modern conglomerate has a reputation problem. After the 1960s-80s conglomerate boom and subsequent bust, Wall Street decided that focused companies create more shareholder value. And for public markets, that's largely true. But for private, founder-led businesses, the conglomerate model has distinct advantages that focused companies cannot replicate.

Shared infrastructure: When eight companies share one accounting team, one legal framework, one technology stack, and one brand identity, the cost per company drops dramatically. Our shared services team supports all eight ventures at roughly 40% of what each company would spend individually.

Cross-selling: Every customer who enters through one venture becomes a potential customer for three others. A student who comes through Abroadiz for study abroad consulting later needs immigration services, then real estate, then potentially AI consulting for their own business. Customer lifetime value multiplies across the portfolio.

Risk distribution: When one vertical faces headwinds, others carry the group. In 2023, when Canadian real estate slowed, our AI consulting and immigration verticals grew 140%. The portfolio remained profitable every quarter because no single market cycle can take down all six verticals simultaneously.

Talent leverage: Top talent wants to work on interesting problems. A conglomerate offers variety. Our best operators rotate between ventures, bringing fresh perspectives and cross-pollinating ideas. The marketing strategies that work for our real estate brand get adapted for our AI products, and vice versa.

The Ecosystem Thesis: Why Random Diversification Fails

The critical distinction between a successful conglomerate and a failed one is the ecosystem thesis. Random diversification — buying a restaurant chain, a software company, and a mining operation — creates no synergy. Each business operates in isolation, and the holding company adds overhead without adding value.

An ecosystem conglomerate is different. Every venture must pass the "two-way value test": Does this new venture create value for existing ventures? Do existing ventures create value for this new venture? If the answer to both isn't yes, we don't build it.

Here's how our ecosystem works in practice:

  • Watts Technologies builds AI tools that power Watts Immigration's document processing, Abroadiz's application matching, and Watts Realty's market analysis
  • Watts Immigration clients who gain PR become homebuyers through Watts Realty and potential entrepreneurs we invest in through Watts Investments
  • Abroadiz students who graduate become Express Entry candidates through Watts Immigration and first-time homebuyers through Watts Realty
  • Watts Community CSR programs build brand trust that benefits every venture's client acquisition

The flywheel effect is real. Each venture makes every other venture stronger. The value of the portfolio is genuinely greater than the sum of its parts.

Capital Allocation Across Verticals

The hardest operational challenge in running a multi-vertical conglomerate is capital allocation. With limited resources and multiple ventures competing for investment, every dollar must go where it creates the most value.

We use a framework we call the "Growth-Maturity Matrix" to allocate capital:

  • Growth-stage ventures (high potential, not yet profitable) receive 60% of available investment capital. They need fuel to reach scale
  • Mature ventures (profitable, stable growth) receive 20% for optimization and defense. They generate the cash that funds growth ventures
  • Experimental ventures (unproven, high-risk) receive 15% with strict milestones. They have 6 months to prove unit economics or get restructured
  • 5% reserve stays liquid for opportunistic moves — a key hire becomes available, a competitor stumbles, or a partnership opportunity appears suddenly

The discipline required is immense. It means saying no to good opportunities in mature ventures to fund great opportunities in growth ventures. It means shutting down experiments that don't hit milestones, even when the team is passionate about them.

Operational Structure That Scales

Running eight companies with one leadership team requires a ruthlessly efficient operational structure. We've iterated on this for five years and landed on a model that balances autonomy with accountability.

The Watts Group operating model:

  • Shared services layer: Finance, legal, HR, IT infrastructure, and brand/design operate centrally. These teams serve all ventures and create consistency
  • Venture-level operators: Each company has a dedicated lead (we call them "Venture Leads") with P&L ownership and full decision-making authority within their domain
  • Weekly rhythm: Every Monday, Venture Leads present one metric, one win, and one blocker. Total meeting time: 45 minutes for all eight ventures
  • Quarterly reviews: Deep-dive into each venture's financials, customer metrics, and strategic alignment. This is where capital reallocation decisions happen

The key insight: decentralize decisions, centralize information. Venture Leads make all operational decisions for their companies. But every metric flows into a shared dashboard that the entire leadership team can see. This prevents siloed thinking while preserving speed.

Hiring and Culture Across Verticals

Culture is the invisible infrastructure of a conglomerate. With 50+ team members across eight ventures, maintaining a unified culture while respecting each venture's unique identity is a constant balancing act.

Our approach has three pillars:

Shared values, different expressions. Every team member, regardless of venture, operates under the same core values: Innovation First, Community Impact, Trust & Integrity, Operational Excellence, Global Thinking, and Relentless Growth. But how those values manifest looks different at an AI company versus a real estate brokerage.

Cross-venture exposure. New hires spend their first week meeting teams from at least three other ventures. Senior team members rotate through other ventures for one-week "tours" annually. This builds empathy, sparks ideas, and prevents the "us vs. them" dynamic that kills conglomerates from the inside.

Unified compensation philosophy. Everyone in the group operates under the same compensation framework. No venture pays dramatically more than another. Equity participation is available across the group, not just individual ventures. This prevents internal competition for talent.

Mistakes We Made (So You Don't Have To)

Building a conglomerate is a masterclass in making mistakes. We've made plenty. Here are the most expensive lessons:

Launching too many ventures simultaneously. In 2022, we tried to launch three ventures in the same quarter. All three suffered because leadership attention was split too thin. We now launch one venture at a time, with a minimum 4-month stabilization period before starting the next.

Underestimating shared services capacity. When venture #5 launched, our finance team was already at capacity serving four companies. Adding a fifth without increasing shared services headcount created bottlenecks everywhere. Now we proactively scale shared services before each new venture launch.

Assuming ecosystem synergy would happen naturally. Just because two ventures could cross-sell doesn't mean they will. We had to build explicit referral systems, shared CRM workflows, and incentive structures to make cross-venture collaboration happen. Synergy is engineered, not assumed.

Not killing experiments fast enough. One venture spent 8 months and significant capital trying to make a market work that simply didn't have product-market fit. The signs were visible at month 3. Emotional attachment to the idea cost us time and money. We now have hard kill criteria built into every experiment.

When Should You Build a Conglomerate?

The conglomerate model is not for everyone. It requires a specific combination of skills, temperament, and market conditions. Build a conglomerate if:

  • You see genuine interconnections between multiple industries that create compounding value
  • You're an operator, not just a strategist — you can build systems that run without your daily involvement
  • You have the discipline to say no to good opportunities that don't fit the ecosystem
  • You can attract and retain Venture Leads who are entrepreneurial enough to run their own company but collaborative enough to work within a larger system
  • You have access to patient capital that understands the 5-10 year timeline for ecosystem effects to fully compound

If any of those don't apply, build a single great company instead. There's nothing wrong with focus. The world needs excellent focused companies far more than it needs mediocre conglomerates.

Looking Ahead: The Next Five Years

Watts Group is five years into what we see as a 20-year build. The ecosystem is operational, the flywheel is turning, and the compounding effects are becoming visible in our numbers. Over the next five years, we're focused on three priorities: deepening each vertical rather than adding new ones, expanding geographically into new markets with our existing model, and building technology that makes the ecosystem effect even stronger.

The conglomerate model isn't the easy path. But for builders who think in systems, who see connections where others see separate industries, and who have the patience to let compounding work, it's the most rewarding path we know.
